Comprehensive guide to commercial pollination services in Queensland, covering legal requirements, hive quantities, equipment, contracts, and crop-specific parameters

Varroa is confirmed in the Lockyer Valley. This changes several legal obligations — read this section carefully before doing anything else.
Step 1 — Registration (mandatory before any commercial activity)
European bees — required
Biosecurity entity registration
Register at business.qld.gov.au before keeping a single European hive. Free for hobbyists, small fee for commercial primary producers. You receive a Hive Identification Number (HIN) — brand at least 1 in every 50 hives. Registrations valid 3 years. Call Biosecurity QLD: 13 25 23.
Native bees — not required
No registration needed for native hives
Stingless bee hives (T. carbonaria, T. hockingsi) do not need registration under QLD Biosecurity Act 2014. However, if you're offering them commercially, you still need an ABN and public liability insurance — see below.
Both bee types
ABN registration
Any commercial pollination activity requires an ABN. Register free at abr.business.gov.au. Required to invoice growers, claim business expenses, and enter formal contracts. Sole trader ABN is fine to start.
Both bee types
Public liability insurance
Non-negotiable for commercial contracts. Growers will ask for your Certificate of Currency before signing. Minimum $10 million cover. Bee-specific policies available through QBA Group Insurance or Marsh Insurance. Expect $800–1,500/year for a small operation. Never place hives on a grower's property without this.
Step 2 — Varroa-specific obligations (currently active in Lockyer Valley)
Mandatory
Monthly hive health checks + Bee 123 reporting
All QLD beekeepers must conduct monthly hive health checks (alcohol wash preferred) and report results — positive or negative — via the Bee 123 form at business.qld.gov.au. This is a legal obligation under the General Biosecurity Obligation (GBO). Records must be kept and available for inspection.
Mandatory
Varroa reporting within 24 hours
If you detect varroa mite in any hive you must report to Biosecurity QLD within 24 hours on 13 25 23. Varroa is currently restricted matter category 2 — reporting is compulsory. Failure to report is an offence under the Biosecurity Act 2014.
Mandatory before moving hives
Pre and post-move hive health checks
You must complete a hive health check before moving hives to any pollination site and after returning them. This is required to avoid spreading varroa to new sites. Document with Bee 123. "Come clean, go clean" — decontaminate all equipment between sites.
Mandatory
Bee biosecurity online training
The QBA strongly recommends (and for commercial operators is effectively mandatory) completing the Bee Biosecurity Online Training course via Plant Health Australia. It's free. Do it within your first 12 months, then repeat every 3 years. Growers may ask for proof of completion.
Step 3 — Business and property obligations
Business
Hive movement record book
Required to record all hive movements in QLD — date, from/to location, number of hives. QLD Apiary Record Book available from QBA. Must be produced on request by biosecurity officers.
Business
Chemical record keeping
All varroa treatments must be recorded including: product name, batch number, hive treated, date, dose. Records must be retained for 2 years. Part of your GBO compliance.
Distances
Minimum apiary distances
QLD Biosecurity Regulation 2016 specifies minimum distances between apiaries. Check with Biosecurity QLD before siting permanent or temporary apiary at a pollination site — commercial distances differ from hobbyist.
Step 4 — Training and qualifications growers expect
Certificate III in Beekeeping (AHC30122)
Offered by TAFE Queensland and some RTOs. Not legally required but increasingly expected by commercial growers when tendering for large contracts. Covers colony management, pest/disease identification, food safety, and business basics. Take it in year 2 once you have practical experience.
QBA membership + bee course
Queensland Beekeepers' Association membership (~$80/year). Attend a beginner course in your first year. Access to the QBA group insurance scheme, contract templates, and the network of commercial beekeepers who are your best source of local knowledge and contract leads.

Native bee hives — commercial parameters
T. carbonaria for commercial use
Used primarily for enclosed crops (strawberries under netting, capsicums, greenhouse tomatoes). Typical placement: 1–3 hives per 500m² of enclosed growing area. Charge: ~$110/hive. Colonies must be active, with visible entrance traffic and at least 12 months old. New colonies are not strong enough for commercial placement.
Minimum viable native fleet
Start with 10 hives minimum for any commercial trial. Scale to 20–50 via splitting before approaching growers. Each hive must be at least 12–18 months old and actively foraging. Don't promise what you can't deliver — a failed hive placement damages your reputation with that grower permanently.
The Lockyer Valley and broader SE QLD grow avocados, macadamias, strawberries, blueberries, cucurbits, stone fruits, and seed crops. All require managed bee pollination. Seasonal overlap creates opportunities to move hives between crops across the year.

Transport equipment — the non-negotiable investment
A full Langstroth hive ready for transport weighs 40–60kg. You cannot move 20+ hives by hand. Transport equipment is where you must spend money — back injury from manual handling is the most common reason beekeepers leave the industry.
Essential — buy early
Hive pallets (4-hive wooden pallets)
The universal standard. 4 hives sit on one pallet (~200kg total). Pallets are forklift-compatible and make loading/unloading fast. Build or buy: 18mm treated ply, 1.2m × 0.6m, with forklift entry slots. Make them yourself for $20 each or buy commercial for $35–60. You need one pallet per 4 hives.
Essential — buy early
Hive straps (ratchet straps)
Strap each hive box and pallet securely before transport. 25mm ratchet straps, 2 per hive during travel, 1 to secure the pallet load. Buy in bulk — $8–15 each. Keep 40+ for a 20-hive operation. Hive boxes must not shift during transit or you risk comb collapse.
Essential — buy early
Transport trailer or ute tray
Starting out (5–20 hives): a heavy-duty box trailer or ute with drop sides carries 10–20 hives per load. 20–60 hives: a flat-top trailer (tandem axle, 7×5m or larger). 60+ hives: you need a truck and dog trailer setup — commercial operators move 120 hives per load on flatbeds.
Essential — buy or hire early
Mini loader / bee loader
The Avant BeePro and MultiOne series are the Australian commercial standard. Lifts 4-hive pallets (~200kg) onto trucks. Articulated chassis handles soft and uneven ground at orchards. Parks sideways on the truck deck. New: $25,000–50,000. Hire: ~$400/day. For 5–20 hives, a hired loader for delivery day is cost-effective. At 50+ hives, own one.
Mid-scale (20–60 hives)
Truck-mounted crane (Hiab)
A crane fitted to a flatbed truck allows one person to load/unload without a separate loader on site. Commercial beekeepers on the Bush Bee Man circuit use this setup. A used Hiab-equipped truck is $40,000–80,000. More versatile for remote or tight access sites where a mini loader can't go.
Small scale only
Two-person hive carriers
Steel frame with handles that grip the hive sides. $80–150 each. Only practical for moving 1–5 hives short distances (e.g., repositioning within a site). Not viable for loading trucks commercially — they are too slow and carry injury risk at scale.
Hive specification for commercial use (Langstroth)
8 or 10-frame Langstroth
10-frame is the QLD commercial standard. 8-frame is lighter and easier to handle alone but lower honey and brood capacity. For pollination work, 10-frame full-depth brood box + 1 super is the industry standard growers expect.
Screened bottom board
Required for varroa management (passive mite drop). Also improves ventilation during transport — critical for preventing overheating on hot days. Build this into every hive from day one.
Entrance reducers
Fit before every transport. Block most of the entrance so bees stay inside during the trip. Mesh entrance reducers allow ventilation. Solid reducers block everything — only use for short moves at night. Remove immediately on placement at the orchard.
Hive identification marking
Brand or paint your HIN on every hive (required by law, at least 1 in 50). For commercial work: also number each hive with a unique ID and photograph every placement. GPS-document where each hive goes. Essential for contract compliance and dispute resolution.
Ventilated moving screens
Replace the solid lid with a mesh screen during transport. Prevents CO2 buildup and overheating. A full pallet of bees generates serious heat — queens can die if poorly ventilated. Commercial suppliers: Beechwood Supplies, B&B Honey Farm (QLD).
Shade netting
Large shade cloth draped over the truck load during transport. Keeps bees contained if any escape entrance blocks, maintains airflow, reduces radiant heat. Must be breathable — not tarps. Standard commercial practice for every hive movement in QLD.
Field and inspection equipment
Commercial bee suit (ventilated)
Inspect and work hives faster in a ventilated suit in QLD summer. Swienty, Sherriff, and Mann Lake make good ventilated commercial suits. Used suits from deceased estates or exiting beekeepers are fine — inspect carefully for holes and wash thoroughly. Never buy used veils — they deteriorate invisibly.
Commercial smoker (large)
A large-capacity smoker (30cm bellows) keeps burning for 2+ hours. Essential for inspecting 20+ hives in a session. Stainless steel, not galvanised. Used is fine. Fuel: hessian, pine needles, dry grass. Keep 2 — they go out at the worst moment.
Hive tools (multiple)
J-style hive tools for frame removal. Buy 5+ — they go missing constantly. Stainless steel, about $15 each new, or $5 used. Sterilise between apiaries with 70% methylated spirits in a spray bottle. Required by GBO when moving between sites.
Alcohol wash kit (varroa monitoring)
500ml jar, methylated spirits, fine mesh sieve, white tray. ~$20 DIY. Do not cut corners here — accurate mite counts are your legal obligation and your contract protection. A high mite count you didn't catch is a failed hive at the grower's site.
Native bee commercial equipment
Commercial OATH or OATH-style boxes
For commercial native bee placement, hives must be robust, watertight, and standardised. All 280×200mm footprint so they stack uniformly for transport. At commercial scale, buy commercially made hoop pine boxes (Quality Beekeeping Supplies, Native Bees Australia) rather than DIY — consistency matters when a grower inspects your hives.
Padded transport box or crate
Native hives are fragile during transport — the soft cerumen comb can shatter if jolted. Foam-lined wooden crates or milk crates with padding protect the colony during movement. Strap individual hives. Do not stack native hives — the weight crushes the boxes.
What growers actually want (and how to prove you deliver it)
Reliability above all
A grower's crop may be worth millions. They need hives on site within a tight bloom window — often 3–7 days before first flowers open. Missing the window or delivering weak hives is a catastrophic outcome for them. Growers talk to each other. One failed delivery ends your local reputation.
Provable colony strength
Growers will ask to inspect colonies on arrival. Be prepared to open any hive on request and demonstrate 6–8 frames of bees. GPS-photographed colony records with frame counts before loading build trust and protect you from post-delivery disputes. Some large growers now use infrared thermal imaging to assess colony strength.
Clean biosecurity documentation
Bring to every delivery: current registration certificate, Bee 123 records for the previous 3 months, pre-move hive health check results, varroa mite treatment records, certificate of public liability insurance. Organised documentation is a differentiator — most small operators have none of this ready.
Written contract every time
Never place hives on a handshake. A one-page contract covering: hive count, strength minimum, delivery/removal dates, fee and payment terms (50% on delivery, 50% on removal is standard), pesticide notification clause, and liability. The QBA and Beeaware.org.au have template agreements.

How to find your first contracts
1
Join Queensland Beekeepers' Association and attend regional meetings
The QBA Lockyer Valley/Toowoomba region meetings are where local commercial beekeepers and growers interact. This is the single most direct route to local contract leads. Attend before you have hives — introduce yourself as building towards commercial work.
2
Contact Lockyer Valley growers directly — start with avocado and strawberry
The Lockyer Valley has a high density of strawberry growers (many under netting — perfect for native bees) and proximity to SE QLD avocado orchards. Cold call or email — a beekeeper who is local, registered, and varroa-compliant is valuable right now because feral bee populations are collapsing. Use the AgriFutures Australia grower directory or simply drive the region and note what's growing.
3
Register with HortConnect / Pollination Australia directories
Pollination Australia (pollinationaustralia.net.au) runs a national directory of registered pollination service providers. Growers use it to find beekeepers. Free to list. Also register with the QBA's beekeeper finder. This is passive lead generation that costs nothing.
4
Approach macadamia growers in Aug–Sep (a year before you want the contract)
Experienced growers book their pollination services in November–January for the following season's bloom. To win a macadamia contract for October next year, start conversations now. Show up in person, bring your biosecurity documents, and offer a trial with a small number of hives.
5
Offer a trial placement on your own farm first
If you grow any fruit or vegetables on your Lefthand Branch property, place your hives there first and document the yield improvement. Photographs, before/after yield data, and a grower testimonial (even from yourself) are powerful proof of concept for your first external pitch.
6
Price to win the first contract, not to maximise
Your first commercial contract is a reference. Offer 10–15% below market rate in exchange for a written testimonial and the right to use the grower as a reference for future contracts. One good reference in the Lockyer Valley opens doors to their neighbours.
Realistic roadmap to commercial pollination — from where you are now
Phase 1 — Foundation (now — 12 months) — build while learning
Build OATH hives (now). Get native bee colony in spring 2026. Register as a biosecurity entity with European bee registration before buying European hives. Complete QBA beginners course and Bee Biosecurity Online Training. Buy 2 European hive setups (VSH stock, screened bottom boards) in spring 2026. Begin monthly Bee 123 reporting. Register ABN. Get public liability insurance. Goal: 2 healthy European hives + 1 native hive by end of year 1. Cost: ~$3,000–4,000 total.
Phase 2 — Build fleet (months 12–24) — split aggressively
Split both European hives in spring 2027 → 4 hives. Split again in spring/summer → 6–8 hives. Split native hive → 2 native hives. Invest in a second-hand box trailer and a set of pallets. Practice pre-move health checks and hive transport. Approach 1–2 local strawberry growers (Lockyer Valley) with native hives — small trial, 3–5 hives under netting. This is your first commercial placement. Document everything. Goal: 8+ European hives, 4+ native hives, 1 trial contract completed.
Phase 3 — First real contracts (months 24–36) — 20–30 hive operation
By year 3 you should have 20–30 European hives via continued splitting (or targeted purchases of strong nucs). This is enough for a small avocado or macadamia contract (5–10 ha). Hire a mini loader for delivery days ($400/day). Pitch 2–3 local growers with full biosecurity documentation package. Use the QBA contract template. Target: 2 paid contracts in the Jun–Oct pollination season at $80–150/hive × 20 hives = $1,600–3,000 per contract. Native bee fleet: 8–10 hives via continued splitting, handling 2–3 strawberry/capsicum contracts year-round.
Phase 4 — Scale to profitability (years 3–5) — 50–100 hive operation
At 50 hives you enter serious small commercial territory. Consider purchasing a used mini loader outright (~$15,000–25,000 second-hand Avant or MultiOne) instead of hiring. Pursue Certificate III in Beekeeping. Stack seasonal contracts: avocado (Jun–Oct) + macadamia (Sep–Oct) + cucurbits (Nov–Mar). At $100/hive average × 50 hives × 3 seasonal placements: ~$15,000/season gross. Deduct transport, treatment, equipment: net ~$8,000–10,000/season as a secondary income. To make it a primary income: you need 150–250 hives and a migratory route.
