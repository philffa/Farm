Why #1
Avocado demand in Australia is insatiable — per-capita consumption has tripled in 10 years. Every new lifestyle block, hobby farm, and suburban acreage owner wants one. Grafted trees (vs seedlings) fruit in 2–3 years instead of 7–10, which is the entire sales pitch. You can produce 4 batches per year in a warm greenhouse. At 1,000 trees/batch × 80% take × $30 wholesale = $96,000 from one greenhouse bay. The rootstock (Zutano or Duke 7) is cheap and fast to germinate. SEQ climate is ideal — Gatton is warm enough year-round to keep production moving.
Graft method
Cleft graft or whip-and-tongue onto 8–12mm diameter seedling rootstock. Graft when rootstock pencil-thick (12–16 weeks from seed). Wrap with grafting tape, cover with clear bag for 3–4 weeks humidity chamber. Take rates improve dramatically with bottom heat (soil at 22–25°C). Most challenging part is timing: scion wood must be from dormant terminal buds, not actively flushing. Once you dial in the method, an experienced grafter can do 200–300/day.
PBR / licensing risk
Standard commercial varieties (Hass, Shepard, Bacon, Reed) are not PBR-protected in Australia — you can graft and sell freely. New releases like "Jala" are PBR-protected — avoid these without a licence.
Gatton-specific advantage
Anderson Horticulture (Australia's largest avocado nursery) works with UQ — meaning there's deep local expertise and rootstock supply nearby. The South East QLD avocado expansion has been significant in recent years, creating demand for commercial orchard volumes as well as retail trees.

Why #2
Macadamia orchards across SEQ and northern NSW are ageing out and being replanted with modern high-yield varieties. Commercial growers need hundreds of trees at a time. Your proximity to the macadamia belt (Gympie, Kenilworth, Nambour) puts you inside the supply catchment. Grafted trees command a massive premium over seedlings — a grafted variety 344 or A4 tree starts producing in 3–5 years vs 8–12 for seedlings, which is the argument that sells itself to a commercial grower. Orchard orders of 200–500 trees at $30–45 each are common.
Graft method
Chip budding or cleft grafting. Chip bud is preferred — higher take rate, less scion material needed. Rootstock from M. tetraphylla seed (readily available, fast germinating). Bud when rootstock 8–15mm diameter. Wrap, leave bud exposed or covered depending on humidity. In Gatton's warm climate, buds push within 3–5 weeks. Scion wood from known commercial varieties — source from reputable QLD macadamia nurseries or AMS (Australian Macadamia Society) members.
Synergy with your property
Your pollination hive operation directly serves macadamia growers. The same growers who book your hives in July–September are also potential buyers for replacement trees. One relationship, two revenue streams.

Note on technique
Lychee is propagated by air layering (marcotting) rather than grafting — it's easier, faster, and has higher success rates. Ring-bark a pencil-thick branch, wrap with moist sphagnum moss and plastic. Roots form in 6–10 weeks. Detach, pot, harden off. Takes more scion tree space than grafting but is forgiving for beginners and produces a true-to-type tree guaranteed to fruit.
Why it ranks highly
Lychee trees are chronically undersupplied in Australian nurseries — good-variety marcotted trees sell out constantly. Fay Zee Sui (early season), Bengal (mid), and Kwai May Pink (late) cover the full harvest window and are what commercial growers want. Gatton's climate is ideal. The Erdon Lee supersized variety commands $100–180 per tree at retail due to extreme scarcity.
Volume consideration
You need established mother trees to take air layers from. Either source large lychee trees to plant as stock plants (1–2 years to establish), or buy them in and start taking layers immediately. 20 good mother trees can produce 200–400 marcots per season.

Why it ranks 4th not higher
Mango grafting has a slightly lower take rate than avocado and requires more skill — the veneer graft or soft-wood cleft graft is technique-sensitive, and mango scion wood must be at exactly the right dormancy stage (panicle/grey wood, not actively flushing). That said, demand is enormous and QLD is the centre of Australian mango culture. Kensington Pride (Bowen) is the workhorse but Honey Gold, R2E2 and calypso-types command $15–25 more per tree retail.
PBR warning
Honey Gold and Calypso are PBR-protected — you cannot graft and sell these without a licence from the variety holder. Kensington Pride, R2E2, and Irwin are not protected. Check AusPBR before grafting any named variety commercially.

The unique advantage here
You already have the scion wood growing in your grove. Zero scion cost. Olive semi-hardwood cuttings also strike without grafting at a reasonable rate, but grafting onto a vigorous rootstock (Frantoio seedling or Arbequina) produces a much faster-establishing tree that commands 3–4× the price of a cutting-grown plant. A grafted tree from a known productive, named variety in your own grove has a genuine origin story — "grafted from a 20-year-old Frantoio at 658 Lefthand Branch Rd" — that adds retail value.
Market
Lifestyle property owners buying 5–50 trees at a time. Council revegetation and streetscape projects (olive as an ornamental). Boutique producers wanting to establish a known variety quickly. Wholesale to garden centres — olive trees are perennial sellers in QLD. Larger form trees (2–3 years in the pot) retail $90–180 each.

Why it's worth doing despite difficulty
Grafted jaboticaba — especially Red Hybrid and Sabara varieties — are almost never available in quantity in Australia. They sell instantly at $80–180 retail and online waiting lists are common. The slow growth means you can hold trees in pots and charge more as they mature. A 3-year-old grafted Sabara in a 30L pot retails $200–350. The market is collectors, serious foodies, and subtropical gardeners — a growing demographic in SEQ. Low volume, very high margin. Treat this as your premium product line alongside volume avocado and macadamia.
2 acres = ~8,100m². The key principle: heated propagation space is your most valuable square metre — use it for grafting and early establishment only. Move hardened-off stock to cheaper shade house or open hardening bays to free up propagation capacity.
QLD summer heat is the design constraint. Your propagation greenhouse must have evaporative cooling or shade cloth rated 50–70% — avocado and macadamia grafts fail above 35°C. Budget $4,000–8,000 for a properly cooled 10×20m greenhouse.

Zone A — Heated propagation greenhouse

~200m² (10×20m) · ~$6,000–10,000

Temperature-controlled (22–32°C). Bottom heat benches. Mist irrigation. This is where grafting and first 6–8 weeks of establishment happen. Capacity: 3,000–4,000 grafted trees at any time across all species. Rotate every 10–12 weeks to Zone B.

Zone B — Shade house hardening

~600m² (20×30m) · ~$3,000–5,000

50% shadecloth, drip irrigation. Trees here for 8–16 weeks post-graft. Lower cost per m² than Zone A. Capacity: 6,000–8,000 trees in 5–15L pots. Can use existing poor shed after minimal upgrade.

Zone C — Stock plant rows (mother trees)

~2,000m² · low cost

Lychee mother trees for air layering (20–30 trees). Olive rootstock and scion rows. Macadamia scion block. Mango scion block. These produce your free scion wood indefinitely. Plant once, harvest forever. Drip irrigated rows.

Zone D — Rootstock nursery beds

~500m² · minimal cost

Direct-sown rootstock seeds (avocado, macadamia, mango polyembryonic). Target: 2,000–3,000 rootstocks in production at all times. In-ground or deep trays. This is your bottleneck if not managed — grafting output is limited by rootstock availability.

Zone E — Grow-on / sales holding

~2,000m² · open air

Hardened trees waiting for sale. Larger-format trees earning premium price with time. Shade cloth optional. Drip irrigation. Can hold 5,000–8,000 trees in 8–25L pots. This space converts to revenue by letting trees size up for 6–18 months at near-zero cost.

Your marketing constraint is getting products to market. The grafting nursery is one of the best enterprises for solving this — trees have multiple buyer types with different price points and almost no cold chain requirements.

🌿 Garden centres — retail wholesale

Bunnings, local independents, and specialist nurseries. Approach with a line sheet and samples. They want consistent supply on 4–6 week replenishment cycles. Margin: they take 40–50%, you get $20–35/tree. Volume: 50–200 trees/order. Closest: Gatton, Ipswich, Toowoomba, Brisbane. 5–8 accounts = $150–250k/yr at scale.

🥑 Commercial orchard developers

Avocado and macadamia orchards being established across SEQ need 200–2,000 trees per order. These are your best single-order customers. Contact via QLD Avocado Growers Association and Australian Macadamia Society. Price: $18–35/tree, massive volume. One order can be $20,000+.

💻 Online direct — highest margin

Shopify or your farm website. Ship in purpose-built plant boxes via Aramex or StarTrack. QLD trees ship nationally with permit. $45–120/tree direct. 200 online orders/month = $9–24k revenue with no middleman. Instagram and YouTube "nursery tour" content drives this — it genuinely works for rare/premium varieties.

🏡 Farm gate / open days

Monthly or seasonal open-nursery days. Customers come to you, pay retail, carry their own tree. Zero freight cost, zero packaging. Combine with the olive harvest experience and honey sales — people who visit for a tree buy $80 of other products. Sell aged premium trees at $120–250 that you couldn't ship.

🌳 Councils and revegetation

Lockyer Valley Regional Council, SEQ catchment authorities, and road corridor planting projects buy trees in volume. Olives, macadamias, and native species for streetscape and revegetation. Quote directly. Slow procurement but large, predictable orders (500–2,000 trees/order). Lower price ($8–18) but guaranteed volume with no marketing effort.
