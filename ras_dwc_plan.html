Section 1

System architecture

The RAS loop handles fish welfare and water quality. The DWC loop handles plant production and nutrient export. They share a sump that acts as the transfer point — RAS effluent feeds DWC, clarified return water tops up the RAS. The two enterprises can be managed, troubleshot, and harvested completely independently.

🪲 BSFL integration: Frass tea (1:20 dilution, 24hr aeration) fed directly into sump — never into RAS tank. DWC beds absorb the nutrient spike. Fish are never exposed to raw frass pathogens.
Why decoupled beats integrated CHOP2 for this farm: In a CHOP2 loop, fish stocking density is capped by plant bed ammonia processing capacity — you can't push fish numbers without adding more grow beds, and vice versa. In this split design, the MBBR biofilter handles the fish-side ammonia independently. You can run higher fish density in the RAS, run higher plant density in the DWC, and optimise each for profit without the other being a constraint.
Section 2

Component specification

Every component is sized for the 8,000L RAS tank and 72m² DWC array. Costs are based on QLD suppliers and Gumtree/Facebook Marketplace second-hand pricing as of 2025.

RAS — fish circuit
🐟
RAS
8,000L round poly tank
2.8m diameter × 1.5m deep. Round tank = no dead corners, uniform flow. 7,500L or 10,000L both fine — use whatever poly tank is cheapest locally. Colorbond green or black preferred — algae suppression.
$800–1,200 new · $350–600 Gumtree
🔄
Solids removal
Radial flow settler, 200L drum
200L HDPE drum with tangential inlet and central standpipe — DIY build, $35 in fittings. Removes ~70% of suspended solids before biofilter. Settled sludge drains directly to frass shelter composting area. Upgrade to drum filter in Year 3 if needed.
$35 DIY · $400–600 commercial drum filter
🦠
MBBR biofilter
200L drum, K3 media 50L fill
Moving bed biofilm reactor — 200L drum half-filled with K3 plastic media, air-lifted. The biofilm converts ammonia → nitrite → nitrate. Surfaces in 4–6 weeks. This is the heart of the RAS. Don't skimp — this is what keeps fish alive at high density.
$120 (drum + media + airline)
☀️
UV steriliser
25W inline UV, 3,000–4,000 L/hr
Kills pathogen load before water returns to fish. Critical for high-density RAS where disease spreads fast. Inline on return line, post-biofilter. Replace bulb annually (~$35). At 8,000L tank, one pass every 2–3 hours is adequate.
$85–120 online (Aqua-Pure or equivalent)
💨
Aeration
80W linear air pump + diffusers
Jade Perch tolerate low DO better than most species but 6–8 mg/L is the target. Two 150mm disc diffusers on the tank floor, one airline to MBBR. Backup battery air pump ($45) for power outages — at high stocking density, 2 hours without aeration is a total loss event.
$65 pump · $25 diffusers · $45 backup
⚙️
Circulation pump
3,000 L/hr submersible
Moves water: RAS tank → radial settler → MBBR → UV → return. Target 1× full tank volume per hour minimum. 3,000 L/hr at 2–3m head. Run continuously on a dedicated circuit — this pump never goes off. Spare pump on shelf at all times.
$65–90 · keep $65 spare on shelf
DWC — plant circuit
🌿
Float beds ×6
2m × 6m × 300mm deep, liner-based
Timber frame (90×45 H3 pine), pond liner, 300mm water depth. Six beds = 72m² total. Build in pairs — one pair per construction weekend. Styrofoam float boards 25mm thick, 50mm net pot holes at 200mm spacing = ~540 plant sites per bed, 3,240 total. Beds inside shed or under shade cloth run off the same roof.
$180–220 per bed DIY · $1,200 total
🪣
Sump tank
2,000L poly, gravity-fed from DWC return
Central reservoir receiving RAS effluent and DWC return water. BSFL frass tea added here (never to RAS). pH and EC monitored in sump — adjustments made once rather than in 6 separate beds. Float valve maintains water level. Sump positioned lower than all beds for gravity return.
$280–380 new · $120–180 Gumtree
🔀
DWC circulation
1× 2,500 L/hr pump, manifold to 6 beds
Single pump in sump feeds all 6 beds via 40mm poly pipe manifold with ball valve per bed for individual flow control. Flood-and-drain timer: 15 min on / 45 min off is sufficient for DWC — roots stay submerged, oxygen exchange happens at surface. Simple, low energy.
$55 pump · $80 pipe and fittings
💡
Supplemental lighting
3× 60W full-spectrum LED bars per bed pair
Only needed for the 2 beds inside the shed (no natural light). The 4 outdoor/greenhouse beds need none. Full-spectrum LED bars at 400–700nm. 16hr photoperiod on timer. At $0.30/kWh this runs ~$180/yr for 2 beds — fully justified by year-round production vs. seasonal.
$55/bar × 6 = $330 · shed beds only
Shared infrastructure
📊
Monitoring
2× digital pH/EC/temp combo meters
One dedicated to RAS (test daily), one to sump/DWC (test every 2 days). Apera or Bluelab — don't buy cheap pH pens for a commercial system. Calibrate weekly with pH 7.0 and 4.0 buffer. Wireless temp/DO logger in RAS tank with alarm below 5mg/L DO.
$85–120 each · $220 total
🌡️
Temperature control
Shade cloth + evaporative cooling in summer
Jade Perch thrive 22–30°C — Lockyer Valley summers rarely exceed this in a shaded shed. Shade cloth over the RAS tank (50%) prevents direct sun heating. In extreme January heat, a $45 evaporative cooler blowing across the tank surface drops temperature 3–4°C adequately. No chiller needed.
$0 (shade cloth already in build) · $45 fan
🔌
Electrical
Dedicated 20A circuit, 4× weatherproof GPO
RAS pump and aeration on one circuit (never shared with lights — pump failure alarm needs to be independent). Lighting on separate circuit with timer. All connections weatherproof IP65 minimum. UPS battery backup ($120) keeps circulation and aeration running 4hrs during power outage — Jade Perch at high density won't survive 2hrs without both.
$380 electrician + $120 UPS
Total infrastructure cost estimate: RAS components $1,500–1,900 · DWC beds + plumbing $2,100–2,600 · Shared infrastructure $1,200–1,600 · Contingency 15% $700–900. Total: $5,500–7,000 installed — significantly cheaper than three separate CHOP2 tanks, more profitable, and more resilient.

Commissioning sequence

Don't put fish in water until the biofilter is established. Don't plant all 6 beds on day one. This sequence keeps complexity manageable and gives each component time to stabilise before the next is added.

Week 1–2 · Jan 2027
Fill and cycle RAS
  • Fill RAS tank with bore water
  • Start circulation pump + aeration
  • Seed MBBR with commercial nitrifying bacteria
  • Dose with ammonia source (fish food)
  • Test NH₃, NO₂, NO₃ daily on whiteboard
  • UV off until biofilter established
No revenue this phase — system cycling
Week 3–4 · Jan 2027
Plant DWC beds 1–2
  • Fill sump with RAS effluent + bore water
  • Plant butter lettuce seedlings, bed 1
  • Plant Thai basil seedlings, bed 2
  • Check EC 1.2–1.8 mS/cm — adjust with fish food
  • pH 6.5–7.0 target — adjust with pH down (citric acid)

Revenue from Week 6: $600–900/month

Section 6

Daily management load

One of the key advantages of this design is how little daily attention it needs once established. The RAS is self-regulating between water changes. The DWC beds need checking but not adjusting most days.

This system is designed to be managed solo alongside BSFL, bees, and olive grove — it cannot demand more than this without compromising those enterprises. If it's taking longer than 20 minutes daily in Month 3+, something is wrong and needs diagnosing, not more management time.
Section 7

Target water parameters

Jade Perch are among the most tolerant aquaculture species for water quality variation — a major reason they suit a small RAS. These are target ranges, not cliffs: brief excursions outside these won't kill fish, but sustained deviation will.

pH — RAS
6.8 – 7.4
Biofilter runs best 6.8–7.2. Below 6.5 → biofilm stress. Above 7.8 → NH₃ toxicity risk.
pH — DWC
6.0 – 7.0
Plants prefer slightly lower. Sump acts as buffer — usually 0.2–0.4 lower than RAS.
Dissolved oxygen
> 6 mg/L
Critical parameter. Below 5 → fish stress. Below 3 → mortality within hours. Alarm set at 5.
Temperature
22 – 30°C
Jade Perch ideal 24–28°C. Below 18 → feed refusal and slow growth. Above 32 → stress.
Ammonia (TAN)
< 1 mg/L
Target < 0.5 after cycling. > 2 mg/L at pH 7+ begins to damage gills. Cause: overfeeding or biofilter crash.
Nitrite (NO₂)
< 0.5 mg/L
Transitional compound — high during cycling, should drop to near-zero once biofilter matures. Monitor weekly.
Nitrate (NO₃)
< 100 mg/L
Plant food — DWC beds consume it. Weekly water change prevents accumulation. Target 20–60 for plant benefit.
EC — DWC sump
1.2 – 2.0 mS/cm
Electrical conductivity = total dissolved nutrients. Below 1.0 = plants hungry. Above 2.5 = nutrient stress.
