This plan assumes a new nuc installed in spring (Oct–Nov, Lockyer Valley). Click any day to see exactly what to do and why.
Varroa is confirmed in the Lockyer Valley (March 2025). You must manage it from day one. The layered approach below is the Queensland DPI recommended integrated pest management (IPM) framework. Mechanical controls go first — chemicals are the last line, not the first.

Layer 3 — Monitoring (monthly, mandatory in QLD)
6
Alcohol wash (gold standard)
Collect ~300 bees (half cup) from the brood nest frames into a jar of methylated spirits. Shake for 60 seconds. Pour through a sieve and count mites on the white surface below. Threshold: act if ≥2% in spring (≥6 mites per 300 bees), ≥3% in summer. This is the most accurate method and required monthly under QLD biosecurity obligations. Report all results via Bee 123 form at business.qld.gov.au.

Layer 4 — Chemical treatments (threshold-triggered, rotated)
Only treat when alcohol wash exceeds threshold. Treating below threshold accelerates resistance. Always rotate modes of action — never use the same chemical twice in a row. Pyrethroid resistance (Bayvarol/Apistan) is already confirmed in SE QLD — do not use pyrethroids.

Oxalic acid — dribble or vapour (Oxybee/Api-Bioxal)
Most effective soft treatment. Only works on phoretic mites (not in capped brood). Best after a brood break or in winter when brood is minimal. Dribble 5ml per seam of bees. Vapour penetrates more evenly. Kills 90%+ of phoretic mites in one application. Available from QLD beekeeping suppliers, now on general permit.

The only soft treatment that penetrates capped brood. Use when brood is present and mites are above threshold. Temperature-sensitive: apply only when daytime temps are 10–29°C — in the Lockyer Valley this means autumn or spring only, not summer. Can stress queens if too hot. Follow label exactly.

Open the hive weekly for the first 3 months. Each inspection should take 20–30 minutes. Always inspect on a warm, sunny, still day (above 20°C). Suit up fully every time until you know your colony's temperament.
Before you open — lighting the smoker
5 min before
Light smoker with dry hessian, cardboard, or wood chips
Smoke must be cool and white, not hot and dark. Test on your wrist — should feel like a warm breath. Hot smoke harms bees. Puff 2–3 times at the entrance, wait 30 seconds. Puff once under the cover before lifting.
Inspection checklist — work through in order
Step 1
Entrance observation (before opening)
Bees flying in with pollen on legs = queen laying, colony active. Dead bees with deformed wings outside = varroa-related DWV virus — act urgently. Bees fanning = hive is warm, normal in summer. Very few bees = investigate.
Step 2
Remove and check supers (if fitted)
Set aside on an upturned lid. Note if honey is being capped — capped = ready to harvest once 80%+ capped. Don't leave frames in direct sun.
Step 3
Check brood pattern
A healthy brood pattern is solid (few empty cells), domed, and tan/brown. Sunken, punctured, or discoloured cappings = disease. Scattered, pepperpot pattern = laying worker (queenless) or sick queen. Photograph any abnormal pattern.
Step 4
Find eggs (or evidence of laying queen)
Eggs look like tiny white grains of rice standing upright in cells, visible only in bright light angled across the frame. If you see eggs, the queen was present within the last 3 days — you don't need to find her every time. No eggs for 2+ inspections = queen issue, investigate.
Step 5
Check stores (honey and pollen)
Frames should feel heavy. A new colony needs at least 2 full frames of capped honey to be comfortable. If stores are low and there's no flow on, feed 1:1 sugar syrup. Pollen should be present in multiple colors near the brood — this is their protein source.
Step 6
Check for swarm cells
Queen cells on the bottom edge of frames = swarm preparation. A peanut-shaped cell anywhere = supersedure (queen replacement). If you see charged (larvae inside) queen cells and the colony is strong, you have 7–9 days before they swarm. Either split now or remove all but one queen cell.
Step 7
Check drone frame and remove if capped
If your drone trap frame has capped drone brood, remove it immediately (bees will have filled it in ~10 days, capped in ~14). Place in a sealed bag in the freezer for 48 hours to kill mites, then return empty frame to hive. Record the date you returned it.
Step 8
Powdered sugar dust
Lightly sift icing sugar over the top bars before replacing the cover. Don't clump it — a fine dusting is enough. Bees clean it off over the next hour, increasing grooming and dropping mites through the screen below.
Step 9
Close up and record
Replace frames in exact order. Replace super. Close hive gently — no banging. Immediately write down: date, weather, queen seen/eggs seen, brood pattern (good/patchy), stores level, swarm cells (y/n), drone frame status, any concerns. Without records you cannot track trends.
Red flags — act the same day
Deformed wings on young bees — varroa-linked DWV virus, do alcohol wash immediately
Foul smell when opening hive — possibly American foulbrood (AFB), do NOT treat yourself, call Biosecurity QLD on 13 25 23
No eggs or brood for 2 consecutive inspections — queen lost or failed, requeen urgently
Rapid population drop with no obvious cause — could be pesticide drift, robbing, or high mite load. Alcohol wash first, then investigate.
